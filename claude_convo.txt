Almost everything in the field exists to either separate the structure from the noise (decomposition) or to model how the past predicts the future (autocorrelation and dependence).
Components
Most time series are thought of as a combination of:

Trend — the long-run direction (growing, shrinking, flat)
Seasonality — a fixed, calendar-driven pattern that repeats at a known period (daily, weekly, yearly)
Cyclical variation — longer, irregular up-and-down swings without a fixed period (e.g. business cycles) — easy to confuse with trend or seasonality, but the period isn't fixed
Residual / irregular — whatever's left over: noise, shocks, one-off events

These combine either additively (observed = trend + seasonal + residual, when seasonal swings stay roughly constant in size) or multiplicatively (observed = trend × seasonal × residual, when swings grow with the level of the series).

VvisualizeVvisualize show_widgetTwo properties matter once you've decomposed a series:

Stationarity — does the mean, variance, and autocorrelation stay constant over time? Most classical models assume this, so non-stationary series get transformed (differenced, log-transformed) until it holds.
Autocorrelation — how strongly does a value depend on its own past? The ACF (autocorrelation function) and PACF (partial autocorrelation function) measure this and are the main tools for figuring out what kind of dependence a series has.

Models
The model families roughly fall into four groups:

Classical / statistical — AR, MA, ARMA, ARIMA (adds differencing for non-stationary data), and SARIMA (adds seasonal terms). These directly model autocorrelation structure and are still the workhorses for well-behaved, single series.
Smoothing methods — simple exponential smoothing (SES), Holt's method (adds trend), Holt-Winters (adds seasonality). The ETS framework formalizes these as a unified set of error/trend/seasonal models.
State-space and Bayesian — the Kalman filter and structural time series models treat the series as a hidden state evolving over time; Facebook's Prophet is a popular additive variant built for business forecasting with holidays and changepoints.
Machine learning / deep learning — gradient boosting with lag and calendar features, RNN/LSTM/GRU networks, and newer architectures like the Temporal Fusion Transformer or N-BEATS. These shine with many related series, long histories, or complex exogenous inputs.

Plot and decompose — look at the raw series and its trend/seasonal/residual split before doing anything else.
Test and fix stationarity — Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) and KPSS tests check for it; differencing, log transforms, or Box-Cox transforms fix it.
Identify structure — ACF and PACF plots suggest how many AR/MA terms (and seasonal terms) to use.
Fit candidate models and select among them — AIC/BIC balance fit against complexity; for ML models, hyperparameter search plays the same role.
Check residual diagnostics — good residuals should look like white noise (no leftover autocorrelation); the Ljung-Box test and a residual ACF plot confirm this.
Forecast with uncertainty — a forecast without a prediction interval is an opinion, not a forecast.
Validate the right way — never use random k-fold cross-validation on time series, since it leaks future information into training. Use walk-forward (rolling-origin) validation instead, and evaluate with metrics like MAE, RMSE, MAPE, or sMAPE.

What you reach for depends on the situation: a single, well-behaved series with limited history usually favors ARIMA/ETS; many related series with rich features favor gradient boosting or deep learning; and anything needing transparent, decomposable forecasts (with holidays, changepoints, business logic) tends to favor Prophet-style additive models or classical state-space methods.You said: so, time series analysis is used toso, time series analysis is used toidentify patterns (seasonality, trend, cycles)forecast future outcomes
